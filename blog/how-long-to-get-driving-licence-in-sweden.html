How Long Does It Take to Get a Driving Licence in Sweden?

A realistic planning guide to how long a category B driving licence can take in Sweden, and what usually speeds up or slows down the process.

May 21, 2026 7 min Nordic Theory Labs

How long does it take to get a driving licence in Sweden?

There is no single official answer to how long it takes to get a Swedish category B driving licence. For one person it may take a few months. For another it may take a year or more. The difference usually comes down to planning, language, previous driving experience, test availability and how often you practise.

This guide gives a realistic way to think about the timeline. It is not a guarantee. Rules, fees and booking availability can change, so always check Transportstyrelsen and Trafikverket before you make final plans.

Why this question matters

Many learners start by asking, “How fast can I get the licence?” A better question is, “What has to be finished before the tests, and how much practice do I need to be safe?”

In Sweden, the licence process is not only paperwork. You need a learner’s permit, an eye test, risk education, theory knowledge and practical driving ability. You also need available test times. If one part is delayed, the whole plan can move.

How the process works in Sweden

Transportstyrelsen handles the learner’s permit, health declaration, eye test registration and supervisor approval for private practice. Trafikverket handles the theory test and the practical driving test.

The normal order is:

  • Apply for körkortstillstånd.
  • Complete the eye test.
  • Start theory study and driving practice.
  • Complete riskettan and risktvåan.
  • Pass the theory test.
  • Pass the driving test.

You cannot judge the timeline only by the test dates. The real timeline is the time it takes to become prepared enough to use those test dates well.

A realistic timeline

For a motivated learner who practises regularly, a common planning window is three to six months. This assumes that the learner’s permit is handled early, lessons are booked consistently, risk courses are not left until the last minute and test availability is reasonable.

If you already have solid driving experience from another country, the timeline may be shorter, but Swedish test expectations can still surprise you. Observation, speed adaptation, environmental driving, cyclists, pedestrians and independent judgement are assessed carefully.

If you are a complete beginner, six to twelve months can be more realistic. You need time not only to control the car, but to make calm decisions in Swedish traffic.

What can speed things up?

The biggest accelerator is regular practice. Driving once every three weeks makes progress slow. Driving several times a week, even shorter sessions, helps your brain turn rules into habits.

Another accelerator is studying theory from the beginning. Many learners wait until they can drive before they study theory. That usually backfires. The theory explains why you should slow down, where to look and how to plan.

Booking riskettan and risktvåan early also helps. These courses must be completed before the tests, and available course times may not match your ideal schedule.

What usually slows people down?

The first delay is paperwork. If the learner’s permit or eye test is not done, you cannot begin properly. If you want to practise privately, the supervisor approval and introductory course also matter.

The second delay is weak theory. A learner may drive fairly well but fail the theory test because they memorised questions instead of understanding the system.

The third delay is overconfidence. Experienced drivers sometimes assume that years of driving abroad mean they are ready. The Swedish test may focus on habits they never had to demonstrate before.

The fourth delay is test availability. In some periods and cities, desirable test times may be limited.

Notes for newcomers and immigrants

If Swedish is not your strongest language, add time for vocabulary. Even if you study theory in another language, the road environment is Swedish. Signs, place names, road markings and practical test instructions are in Swedish.

Build a small driving glossary early: höger, vänster, parkera, vänd, väjningsplikt, övergångsställe, motorväg and landsväg. Use those words during practice, not only while studying.

Common mistakes

The most common mistake is setting a test date before you know your actual level. A date can motivate you, but it can also create pressure that leads to bad preparation.

Another mistake is taking too many lessons without practising between them. Lessons are for feedback. Repetition is where the skill becomes stable.

A third mistake is treating risk courses as the final step. They are easier to schedule when you plan them calmly, not when the theory test is already booked.

Cost, time and planning notes

Time and cost are connected. If you practise privately with an approved supervisor, you may reduce the number of paid lessons. If you rely only on driving school, progress may be more structured but more expensive.

A useful plan is to divide the process into four phases: permission and setup, basic control, traffic judgement, and test readiness. Do not move to the last phase until your instructor or supervisor sees stable behaviour, not just one good drive.

Short summary

A Swedish category B licence may take a few months for a well-prepared learner, but many people need six months or more. Start with the permit and eye test, study theory early, practise often and do not book tests before you are genuinely ready.

Frequently asked questions

Can I get a licence in one month?

It may be possible in unusual cases, but it is not a safe planning assumption. Permit processing, course dates, test availability and real skill level all matter.

Should I book the theory test early?

Book when you have a study plan and can finish preparation before the date. Remember that a passed theory result is valid for a limited time.

What takes the longest?

For most learners, practical driving confidence and safe traffic judgement take the longest.

Where should I check current rules?

Use Transportstyrelsen for permits and licence rules, and Trafikverket for tests and booking details.
