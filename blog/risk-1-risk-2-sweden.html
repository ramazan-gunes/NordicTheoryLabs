Risk 1 and Risk 2 in Sweden: what learners need to know

Risk training is one of the compulsory parts of getting a category B driving licence in Sweden. Many learners hear the names Risk 1 and Risk 2 early in the process, but they are often unsure when to book them, what happens during the courses and whether they need to study for them like a normal test.

A practical guide to Sweden's compulsory risk training for category B: what Risk 1 and Risk 2 cover, when to book them and how to avoid timing mistakes.. Use it to connect theory, practice and safe decisions before the Swedish driving test.

What is risk training?

For a category B licence, Sweden requires a completed and valid risk course before the Trafikverket driving tests. The course is not one single lesson. It has two parts:

  • Part 1, often called Risk 1, is a theoretical risk course.
  • Part 2, often called Risk 2, is a practical risk course.

The purpose is not to teach you a list of exam answers. The point is to help you understand how serious crashes happen and how everyday choices can create dangerous situations.

Risk 1: behaviour, alcohol, drugs and fatigue

Risk 1 focuses on human behaviour. The course covers alcohol, other drugs, fatigue and other high-risk behaviour in traffic.

For many learners, this part feels more like a guided discussion than a school exam. You talk about decisions, attitudes, peer pressure, distraction, speed choices and how tiredness affects judgement. The course is especially useful if you connect it to real driving: late evenings, stress, passengers, unfamiliar roads and the feeling that you are "almost ready" before you actually are.

Risk 2: speed, safety and difficult conditions

Risk 2 is the practical part. For car learners, it is commonly associated with slippery-surface training, but it is broader than just skidding. The official focus is speed, safety and driving in certain conditions.

The lesson helps you experience how speed affects braking distance, steering, control and margins. The value is not that you learn to perform tricks on a slippery track. The value is that you feel how quickly control can disappear when speed, surface and reaction time work against you.

When should you book Risk 1 and Risk 2?

Do not leave the risk courses until the last week before your test. At the same time, do not take them so early that you cannot connect the lessons to your actual driving experience.

A good planning rule is this:

  • Book Risk 1 once you understand the basic driving licence process and have started theory study.
  • Book Risk 2 near the later part of your practical training, when you can handle the car safely and understand normal traffic situations.

Trafikverket's own step-by-step guidance places the risk course near the end of training. That is sensible: the courses become more meaningful when you already have some experience to compare with.

How long is the risk course valid?

A completed risk course is valid for five years, but the validity ends on the same day your driving licence is issued. If your licence has been revoked and you must take the tests again, you generally need to complete the risk course again before testing.

Because rules and provider routines can change, always check the current information with Transportstyrelsen and your course provider before booking.

Is Risk 1 or Risk 2 a pass-fail test?

For most learners, the risk courses should not be treated like a theory test. The important thing is that you attend, participate and complete the course with an authorised provider. The provider reports completion so it can be registered for your driving licence process.

Still, you should take the training seriously. If you arrive unprepared, tired or unable to participate safely, the provider may not be able to approve your completion.

Common mistakes

The first mistake is booking Risk 2 too early. If you cannot control the car confidently, you may not get the full value from the practical course.

The second mistake is assuming the course is the same for all licence categories. The car risk course is for category B. Motorcycle risk training is different, and the motorcycle course does not count for car learners.

The third mistake is forgetting timing. If your risk course is not registered and valid when you take the tests, your plan can fall apart even if your theory knowledge and driving are ready.

A simple plan

Put the risk courses into your driving plan early. Ask your driving school when they recommend Risk 1 and Risk 2 based on your current level. If you practise privately, check authorised providers yourself and book with enough margin before the tests.

Risk training is not just an administrative box. Done at the right time, it helps you understand why Swedish driving education talks so much about margins, speed, judgement and responsibility.
